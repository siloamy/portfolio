Context

The City of Vancouver asked us to explore why people in Vancouver do not prepare for emergencies, such as natural disasters, when they know they should. From our findings we determined one cause to be individuals not seeing value in themselves, and as a result, neither in preparing for emergencies. Therefore we proposed Refuge, a platform for individuals to talk anonymously about their struggles, to help individuals rediscover their self-worth.

Process Analysis

We consolidated our research into a Synthesis Wall and dove deeper into why through an Affinity diagram. From these methods, we synthesized a primary, and two secondary, insights.

Primary Insight

Second generation students are preoccupied with proving their value in having a place in the world to be thinking about protecting what they have.

Secondary Insight

Second generation students perceive their worth by what they achieve in comparison to their peers because work and school systems prioritize people based on their achievements.

Secondary Insight

They are driven to prove themselves to society, and in turn their families, in attempt to secure the feeling of acceptance. They feel honour-bound to repay their parents’ sacrifices that provided them with opportunities.

We asked ourselves where and how people learned their value, and arrived at human connection and interaction. To help us generate solutions, we posed this question: How might we foster human connections in an emotionally-safe environment, and through that connection, reshape their perception of self-value?

Reflection

From this project, I learned the importance of speaking to the people throughout the design process. Their experiences and mindsets are essential to creating something that actually helps your audience. Additionally, they broaden and challenge your own perspective and thoughts, leaving you more open-minded than when the project began.
